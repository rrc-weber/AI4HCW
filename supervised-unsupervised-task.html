How Do Machines Learn?

Before a machine can recognise a tumour on a scan or flag a deteriorating patient, it has to learn — and there are fundamentally different ways that learning can happen. In this task you'll experience both.

Part 1 — Supervised Learning

You provide labelled examples. The algorithm learns to map inputs to correct outputs. Like learning with an answer key.

Part 2 — Unsupervised Learning

No labels are given. The algorithm finds its own patterns and groupings in the data. No answer key — just structure.

What just happened?

You experienced the core loop of supervised machine learning.

When you labelled those 8 animals, you were creating training data — pairs of (input, correct label). The algorithm found patterns that separate the categories: shape of ears, body form, presence of a beak. It then applied those patterns to animals it had never seen.

✅ What went right

Three of the four test animals were classified correctly with high confidence. The algorithm generalised successfully from your 8 examples.

⚠️ What went wrong

Animal #12 was misclassified — labelled as a cat (61% confidence) but actually a dog. Small training sets and similar-looking cases cause errors.
Key vocabulary:
Training data — labelled examples the algorithm learns from.
Generalisation — applying learned patterns to new, unseen data.
Confidence score — the algorithm's certainty in its prediction (not the same as accuracy).
Misclassification — an error where the predicted label doesn't match the true label.

In clinical AI, supervised learning is used to train models to detect cancer on imaging, predict deterioration from vital signs, or flag abnormal lab results — but all of these require large amounts of carefully labelled clinical data before training can begin.

The important limitation: you can see that 🐦 in Group A — one bird was clustered with the cats because its compact, round body looked more similar to the cats than to the other birds. No human told the algorithm that birds and cats are different species. It only had shapes. Unsupervised learning is powerful for exploring unknown data — but its groups don't always map onto the categories humans care about. In medicine, this matters: an algorithm might cluster patients by lab values into groups that don't correspond to any recognisable diagnosis, or that reflect data collection biases rather than real disease patterns.

Supervised vs Unsupervised

Two fundamentally different approaches to machine learning — and different use cases in clinical AI.

🏷 Supervised Learning

Requires labelled training data
Learns to predict specific known categories
Output is a label or value a human defined
Performance measurable against ground truth
e.g. Diabetic retinopathy detection, sepsis prediction

🔍 Unsupervised Learning

No labels needed — uses raw data
Discovers structure the human didn't pre-define
Output is groups or patterns — needs interpretation
Harder to validate — no 'correct answer'
e.g. Patient phenotyping, genomic clustering, anomaly detection

🏥 Why does this matter for you?

When you encounter a clinical AI tool, one of the most important questions is: how was it trained? A supervised model trained on labelled scans from one hospital may not generalise to your patient population. An unsupervised model grouping patients may have found clusters that have no clinical meaning — or that reflect data collection biases rather than disease. Knowing the difference between supervised and unsupervised learning helps you ask better questions of the people deploying these tools.
