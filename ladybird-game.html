🔑 The Key Insight

The AI has 80% sensitivity and 90% specificity, with only 20% of ladybirds actually having 7 spots. What does that really mean for each positive result?

Positive Predictive Value (PPV) — how often a positive AI result is actually correct:
PPV = (Sensitivity × Prevalence) ÷ [(Sensitivity × Prevalence) + ((1 − Specificity) × (1 − Prevalence))]
PPV = (0.80 × 0.20) ÷ [(0.80 × 0.20) + (0.10 × 0.80)] = 0.16 ÷ 0.24
PPV = 67% — only 2 in 3 AI positives are real 7-spotted ladybirds!

This means if you always agree with a positive AI result, you'll be wrong about a third of the time. The AI's accuracy matters — but so does the prevalence of what you're looking for.

Negative Predictive Value (NPV) — how often a negative AI result is actually correct:
NPV = (Specificity × (1 − Prevalence)) ÷ [(Specificity × (1 − Prevalence)) + ((1 − Sensitivity) × Prevalence)]
NPV = 95% — negative results from this AI are very reliable.

When prevalence is low, the AI is very good at ruling things out — but less good at ruling them in.

🏥 In Clinical AI

Replace "7-spotted ladybird" with "diabetic retinopathy" or "lung nodule" — the maths is identical. An AI with 80% sensitivity and 90% specificity screening a low-prevalence population will generate a lot of false positives. Understanding PPV and NPV is essential for any clinician working with AI diagnostic tools.
