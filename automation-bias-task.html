    The Three Critical Cases

    What Is Automation Bias?

    Automation bias is the tendency to over-rely on automated systems — to accept machine recommendations without adequate critical evaluation. In clinical settings, this means a clinician may accept an AI's output even when the clinical evidence in front of them tells a different story.

    It is not about distrust of AI. It is about maintaining the habit of looking at the actual patient data rather than the system's summary of it.

    📊 In a 2024 study of 28 trained pathology experts using AI clinical decision support, overall diagnostic performance improved with AI assistance — but the same AI integration produced a 7% automation bias rate, where clinicians overturned their own initially correct evaluations in response to erroneous AI advice.

    Crucially, time pressure did not increase how often automation bias occurred — but it did increase its severity, with greater reliance on the AI's incorrect outputs and a corresponding decline in performance.

    Rosbach E, Ganz J, Ammeling J, Riener A, Aubreville M. Automation Bias in AI-Assisted Medical Decision-Making under Time Pressure in Computational Pathology. arXiv:2411.00998 [cs.HC]. 2024.

    Questions for Reflection

    💬 Take a moment to consider:

    1. For each case, did you read the clinical findings first — or did you read the AI recommendation first? Does the order matter?
    2. The AI panel is designed to look authoritative. Did its visual design influence you? What does that tell you about how AI should be presented in clinical systems?
    3. In three of these cases, the AI was dangerously wrong. But it was right in seven. How do you maintain appropriate scepticism without dismissing the tool's genuine value?
    4. If you accepted a dangerous recommendation: could time pressure alone explain it, or were there other factors?
