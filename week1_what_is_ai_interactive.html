"AI" is one word doing a lot of jobs

You hear it everywhere — in the news, from suppliers, from colleagues. But "AI" is an umbrella term covering very different technologies. Some are decades old and sit quietly in systems you already use. Others arrived in the last few years and are still being tested.

At its simplest: artificial intelligence is software that performs tasks we'd normally expect to need human intelligence — spotting patterns, understanding language, making predictions, or generating content.

💡 A useful picture: think of most modern AI as a very fast, very thorough pattern-matcher. It learns by example, finding signals in large amounts of data that a person might miss or take far too long to spot by hand.
The one distinction that explains everything

Before the jargon, there is a single split that the rest of this step builds on: who wrote the rules?

📋 Rules written by a human

A person decides the logic in advance and codes it in. Predictable and easy to audit.

IF NEWS2 score ≥ 5 → escalate

This is a rule-based system. The computer never "learns" — it just follows instructions.

🧠 Rules learned from data

Instead of being told the rules, the software is shown thousands of examples and works out the patterns itself.

Show 100,000 X-rays → learn what pneumonia looks like

This is machine learning — the engine behind almost all the "new" AI.

Hold onto this. Everything in the next section is really just answering: did a human write the rule, or did the machine learn it — and from what?

Three things to keep in mind
🚫 AI is not conscious or self-aware. It has no understanding, goals, or feelings. It processes patterns — nothing more.
🚫 AI does not always know when it is wrong. It can produce confident, fluent, incorrect answers. That is a patient-safety issue, not a quirk.
🚫 AI is not a replacement for clinical judgement. It is a tool. You remain responsible for the care you provide.

Key takeaways
📋
The core split: did a human write the rule (rule-based, like NEWS2) or did the machine learn it from data (machine learning, like Deep Medical)?
🪆
They're nested, not rival. Deep learning sits inside machine learning and powers most computer vision, NLP and generative tools.
🏥
It's already here. DERM, e-Stroke, Mia, GI Genius, Deep Medical, C2-Ai and ambient scribes are real NHS examples — not science fiction.
🎯
All NHS clinical AI today is narrow — brilliant at one task, unable to generalise. General AI does not exist.
⚕️
Responsibility stays with you. Knowing the type of AI helps you ask the right safety and validation questions.
