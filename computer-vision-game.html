Images Are Just Numbers

To a computer, an image isn't a picture — it's a grid of numbers, one per pixel. Computer vision finds features by sliding small filters across that grid. Here's a simple "scan" with a bright lesion on it. Watch the filter trace its edges.

1 · A picture is a grid of numbers

2 · Slide a filter to find features

A filter (or kernel) is a tiny grid of weights. It slides over every position; at each spot it multiplies overlapping numbers, adds them up, and writes one number to the feature map. Different filters reveal different features.

What you just saw

🔢
No "seeing" involved. The image is pure numbers. Everything a vision model does is arithmetic on those numbers.
🔎
Filters extract features. One filter finds vertical edges, another finds horizontal ones, another blurs. Stack hundreds of them and a network learns to detect textures, shapes, then whole structures.
🧠
This is the first layer of a vision network. Tools like Skin Analytics DERM or Brainomix e-Stroke begin exactly here — then feed these feature maps into the deep-learning layers you met earlier.
⚖️
It only knows the pixels it's given. If the training images differed from your patients' scans, the same filters can pick up the wrong things — which is why validation matters.
