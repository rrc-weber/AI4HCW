Watch a Neural Network Fire

"Deep" learning just means many layers of simple units called neurons. Each neuron adds up its inputs, weighted, and "fires" a little or a lot. Set a patient's signs, watch the signal flow through the layers — then train the network and see it get smarter.

1 · Set the inputs

Four signs go into the network. Each is just a number (0 = normal, 1 = abnormal). Toggle a few, then run a forward pass.

2 · The forward pass

Press Forward pass. The signal travels left to right: every connection carries a weight, each neuron sums what it receives and fires (brighter = more active). The final neuron outputs a deterioration risk.

What you just saw

🔵
A neuron is simple. It multiplies each input by a weight, adds them up, and squashes the result into an "activity" between 0 and 1. Nothing more.
🧱
"Deep" = stacked layers. Early layers detect simple things; later layers combine them into more abstract judgements. Real medical imaging networks have dozens of these layers and millions of weights.
🎓
Learning = tuning weights. Training nudges every weight a little, over and over, to shrink the error. The "knowledge" of a deep learning model lives entirely in those numbers.
❓
That's also why it's hard to explain. The answer is spread across thousands of weights, not a rule you can read — the opposite of a rule-based system.
